**Historical Context: The RRP as Monetary Plumbing**

The Overnight Reverse Repo facility was established in 2013 as a modest backstop. Its job was simple: provide a floor for short-term rates. Then 2020 happened. The pandemic response injected $5+ trillion into the economy, and the RRP became the largest overnight parking lot in financial history.

At its December 2022 peak, it held $2.556 trillion. That was:
- 38% of the Fed's total balance sheet ($8.9 trillion)
- 10.5% of U.S. GDP
- 2.1x the excess reserves of the five largest U.S. banks
- The single largest "we have nowhere else to put this" liquidity buffer ever constructed

The RRP performed three stabilization functions:

1. **Rate floor enforcement:** Kept money market rates from collapsing below Fed targets
2. **Treasury supply absorption:** When issuance surged, MMFs could rotate into RRP instead of forcing T-bill yields to zero
3. **Balance sheet shock absorber:** When dealers faced constraints, RRP gave cash somewhere to go that wasn't "break the repo market"

All three are now gone. The system is driving without airbags.

**Term Premium Decomposition: Why 70 BPS Is Dead**

Term premium is the extra yield investors demand to hold long-duration bonds. It compensates for interest rate risk, inflation uncertainty, and the fact that you'll need to find a buyer someday when issuance is $3.5T annually.

Historically, 10Y-2Y term premium averaged 70-90 bps. By late 2024, it compressed to 50-60 bps despite:
- Debt-to-GDP rising from 80% to 124%
- Foreign official demand declining from 40% to 14% of new issuance
- Deficits structurally above 5% of GDP

Why? Three mechanical reasons:

1. **Fed balance sheet support:** Even during QT, the Fed held $4.3T of Treasuries. The market assumed the Fed wouldn't allow disorderly repricing. That assumption is now being tested.

2. **Foreign central bank reinvestment:** While net purchases declined, foreign central banks kept reinvesting maturing holdings, absorbing 30-40% of new issuance. That's ending as Japanese lifers finally get positive yields at home.

3. **Pension/insurance regulatory demand:** Liability-driven investors are *required* to hold long-duration bonds. But their incremental demand is now fully absorbed‚ÄîBaby Boomer payouts are beginning, and high equity valuations tempt asset allocation shifts elsewhere.

**The Three Demand Pillars Are Cracking:**

**Pillar 1‚ÄîFed Balance Sheet:** QT ends, but the Fed won't *expand* the balance sheet without a crisis. The $2.3T reduction removed significant price support. The composition shift matters more‚Äîmore MBS, fewer Treasuries.

**Pillar 2‚ÄîForeign Official Demand:** Japan's Treasury holdings are down $180B year-over-year as 10Y JGB yields hit 1.15% (up from 0.25% in 2023). ### Layer 3: Market Structure‚ÄîDealer Balance Sheet Constraints and Basis Compression

**The Role of Primary Dealers in Treasury Market Microstructure**

There are 24 primary dealers. They are *required* to bid at every Treasury auction. They must provide continuous two-way markets. They must absorb supply/demand imbalances.

They hold approximately $80-120 billion in net Treasury positions. That sounds large until you realize:
- $28 trillion outstanding / $100B dealer inventory = **0.36% of market**
- $640 billion daily volume / $100B inventory = **6.4 days of trading volume**

When Treasury issuance surges quarterly, dealers must temporarily absorb $40-80B of *incremental* supply. For 1-4 weeks, this strains balance sheets until securities are distributed.

**The Balance Sheet Constraint: SLR and the Zero-Return Problem**

Basel III's Supplementary Leverage Ratio requires banks to hold Tier 1 capital equal to 3-5% of *total assets*, risk-weighted or not. A $100M Treasury position (zero credit risk) requires the *same* capital as a $100M corporate loan (material credit risk).

The incentive is perverse: hold 3.95% yielding Treasuries and consume scarce balance sheet capacity, or hold 7-8% corporate loans and earn higher ROE. Dealers are rational. They choose the latter.

**The Transmission Mechanism: From Dealer Stress to Cross-Asset Contagion**

When dealer balance sheets are constrained, three pathways activate:

**Pathway 1‚ÄîRepo Rate Spikes:**

**Pathway 3‚ÄîBasis Trade Unwinding:**

Unwinding means selling cash Treasuries, buying back futures‚Äîwidening basis spreads and creating forced selling in cash markets. The 10Y cash-futures basis widened from -8 bps to -35 bps in October-November 2025. Unwinding is already underway.
